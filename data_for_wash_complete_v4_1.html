Regression risk: Levels 3–5 are all vulnerable to reversal on ministerial change, donor exit or fiscal shock (Ramanathan et al., SSIR 2026). Level 6 is the first level where the system is resilient to all three. A maturity score should always be dated and contextualised — a Level 4 score in year 1 of a new government is worth less than the same score in year 4 of the same administration.

Ⅲ · Appendix Concepts
Key terms, evidence sources, and critical assumptions grounding this Theory of Change.
System layers
Policy layer
Layer 01
The national governance and standards environment. Now explicitly includes indicator harmonisation — aligning sector definitions with JMP/SDG6 standards. Misaligned indicators across sub-systems is the single most commonly documented failure in national WASH monitoring. None of the 21 ESA countries had fully harmonised indicators (Harris & Pearce, 2021).
System layer
Layer 02
The digital infrastructure and data compilation layer. The UNICEF/IRC guideline (2023) identifies this as the hardest problem: not collecting data, but deliberately compiling, aggregating, and analysing it from multiple sub-systems into a coherent national picture. Split into two explicit activities: platform deployment and cross-system KPI compilation.
Community layer
Layer 04
The downward accountability layer. Includes formalised grievance channels and user surveys as specific activities. Mulgan (2011): "transparency alone, with no prospect of correction, falls short of full accountability." The programme must verify that regulatory or contractual consequences exist for grievances — not only a mechanism to receive them.
New concepts in v2
Data compilation
New · Layer 02
The deliberate process of bringing together KPI data from multiple independent sub-systems into a unified national picture. The UNICEF/IRC guideline (2023) defines this as the core function of the national WASH monitoring system — and the step most commonly missing even when individual sub-systems are functional.
Indicator harmonisation
New · Layer 01
Alignment of indicator definitions across sub-systems so data from different sources can be meaningfully compiled and compared. Required for the system layer's compilation mechanism to function. Failure to harmonise was documented in all 21 ESA countries assessed — including systems designed specifically for national monitoring.
Monitoring maturity
Impact frame
A six-level ladder from absent sub-systems to demonstrated service improvement (UNICEF/IRC, 2023, Figure 5). The impact statement in this ToC has been reframed from "universal WASH access" — unattributable at programme level — to "countries advance 1–2 maturity levels", which is specific, measurable, and honest about causal reach within 5 years.
Data use typology
Purpose frame
Kuster & Batjes (2017) identify four monitoring purposes: policy making, strategic management, operational management, knowledge generation. The UNICEF/IRC guideline adds accountability dimensions: upward (to ministers, funders), sideward (to sector peers), downward (to citizens). Each layer of this ToC serves a different combination of these use types.
Evidence base
UNICEF / IRC (2023)
Primary
Guideline for Strengthening National Systems to Monitor Water, Sanitation and Hygiene Services. UNICEF NYHQ and IRC International Water and Sanitation Centre. Defines the national WASH monitoring system framework, six-level maturity model, and assessment methodology. Directly grounds all five layers and the compilation activity of this ToC.
Harris & Pearce (2021)
Regional study
Understanding monitoring for SDG6 across Eastern and Southern Africa. UNICEF WASH Technical Paper TP 17/2021. Assessment of 21 ESA countries. Key findings: none fully monitor progress against set targets; 12 of 21 have no routine hygiene data; none have fully harmonised JMP indicators; only 6 fully integrate utility data.
GLAAS (2019)
Global survey
National systems to support drinking-water, sanitation and hygiene: global status report 2019. WHO / UN-Water. All responding countries reported some WASH targets aligned with SDG6, but data required to assess progress are not always collected. The target for UNICEF's 2022–25 Strategic Plan is 25 countries achieving all four monitoring sub-indicators; as of 2021 no country had met all four.
Critical assumptions
Decision-makers use accessible data
Cross-cutting
The programme's overarching assumption. UNICEF (2023) identifies persistent mismatch between data producers and key data users, and lack of motivation and capacity to interpret data. The most frequently violated assumption in WASH monitoring globally. The programme must include specific use-enabling activities — not assume use happens automatically once data is available.
Systems maintained post-project
Sustainability
Ethiopia's WASH M&E MIS (developed since 2008) and Ghana's Sector Information System (since 2010) had both not resulted in functional systems as of 2023, despite significant external investment. The most successful systems were designed around existing institutional capacities. This assumption requires explicit handover plans, domestic budget lines, and government ownership from design stage.
Grievance mechanisms have consequences
Community layer
A complete accountability relation requires three stages: information, discussion, and consequences (Mulgan, 2011). Grievance channels that stop at transparency do not achieve the downward accountability this layer aims for. The programme must verify the regulatory or contractual framework exists to act on grievances, not only to receive them.
